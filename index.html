Our Progress so far :

We as team AGV dream to build an autonomous car. This is viewed as replacing the manual driver with an autonomous system capable of navigating the road. An integral part in the realisation of this dream is perception. Computer vision, being the backbone of perception, has been a major focus of our team. Our major objectives have been :-
1. Removal of visible obstacles and potential dangers to the obstacle
2. Extraction of lane boundaries from the input image to ensure the vehicle positioning wrt road
3. Removal of remaining features that are not required for the safe locomotion of the vehicle
4. Creation of occupancy grid map of the surrounding.
Given the environment we are working on is dynamic, we need to experiment with various techniques for feature extraction and noise removal. Most of our work has been in developing intensity invariant algorithms and working on region independent algorithms for feature extraction. Our workflow so far has been the following :-



As we tried on different algorithms and channels, we found out that the HSV colour system is most favoured to remove obstacles for IGVC setup. Also, the lane segments are best retrieved for BGR color system. The conversion of image view from perspective to top requires a matrix transform of the image by multiplying it with a transformation matrix. Also, the input image may contain information above the horizon on some occasions.The team devised a unique sky-removal algorithm to deal with this issue and this increased processing speeds by at least 25% . The algorithm works on column-wise standard mean deviation in order to determine the boundary at each column and then determines the horizon line by averaging out the boundary points at each column. For lane segment extraction, blue and red channels were split, threshold value calculated and finally merged. This gave least noise and best retention of lanes. The lane classification required nearest-neighbour algorithm and it classifies each lane segment as left or right part. Then , we tried some lane-fitting algorithms to fill in the gaps between lane segments of same side. But since, curve regression was a very costly operation, we simply did straight line approximation of the road in close proximity of present bot location.

Challenges faced:-


            1. Obstacle removal- The algorithm is limited to obstacles of certain color, such as the cones and barrels used to indicate diversion in roads, can only be removed from the image. The algorithm is intensity invariant and thus does not depend on minor light changes. But the limitation of color required a separate obstacle detector , LIDAR.
             2. Grass Removal- Algorithms based on certain color characteristics and image features do not work well universally due to presence of different type of dry grass in different areas. So, machine learning was used on the intensity invariant form of the image. This gave better results although the performance of the system was compromised.
            3. Lane segmentation- Curve regression to complete broken lanes was very costly in terms of time and space. So, we used linear approximation and setting a dynamic target according to the immediate curvature of the lane. Also the image was split into various parts vertically and the part-wise mean points of each parts were used for regression.This gave better results at low cost.
            4. Transformation matrix- The transformation matrix is subject to angle of inclination and position of the camera. Hence, every time the parameters are changed, it needs to be calibrated properly.


The Road Ahead

We plan to work on motion analysis in the recent future. Several tasks relate to motion estimation where an image sequence is processed to produce an estimate of the velocity either at each points in the image or in the 3D scene, or even of the camera that produces the images . Examples of such tasks are:

  • Tracking – following the movements of a (usually) smaller set of interest points or objects (e.g., vehicles or humans) in the image sequence.
  • Optical flow – to determine, for each point in the image, how that point is moving relative to the image plane, i.e., its apparent motion. This motion is a result both of how the corresponding 3D point is moving in the scene and how the camera is moving relative to the scene.
        We are planning to come up with proper system and simulation software to test our algorithms and plans before testing it during real-time testing on the bot.

Localization And Slam

Localization And Slam

INTRODUCTION



Robot localization denotes the robot's ability to establish its own position and orientation within the frame of reference. Localization involves one question: Where is the robot now? Or, robo-centrically, where am I, keeping in mind that "here" is relative to some landmark (usually the point of origin or the destination) and that you are never lost if you don't care where you are. All localization techniques generally provide two basic pieces of information:
1.What is the current location of the robot in some environment?
2.What is the robot’s current orientation in that same environment?

In order to track the current position of the robot, It uses sensors which give data along with errors.cAs the environment doesn’t behave ideally as we expect which further adds on to the error of the sensor data. So, numerous filters are used to minimize the erroneous results and approximately predict the position of the robot. The commonly used filters are:
1.Bayes Filter
2.Kalman Filter
3.Extended Kalman Filter
4.Particle Filter

Kalman Filter is one of the most widely used filters in mobile robotics. It is a technique from estimation theory that combines the information of different uncertain sources to obtain the values of variables of interest together with the uncertainty in these.The filter has been successfully applied in many applications, like missions to Mars, and automated missile guidance systems.




Implementation of localization requires a system and measurement models that are needed by the filter which are:

  • A model of the Robot Kinematics
  • A GPS-like sensor
  • A landmark-based sensor.


SLAM



Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM) is a method of determining the location of a vehicle in an entirely unknown environment and simultaneously mapping the environment. Its usage has grown exponentially over the recent years. It has been used in unmanned aerial vehicles, autonomous underwater vehicles, planetary rovers, newly emerging domestic robots and even inside the human body. SLAM consists of multiple parts:
  • Landmark extraction
  • Data association
  • State estimation
  • State update
  • Landmark update
SLAM is applicable for both 2D and 3D motion. Mapping deals with the questions "What does the world look like ?" and localization deals with the question "Where am I ?".

SLAM deals with the problem of building a model leading to a new map, or repetitively improving an existing map, while at the same time localizing the robot within that map. SLAM will always use several different types of sensors to acquire data with statistically independent errors. Statistical independence is the mandatory requirement to cope with metric bias and with noise in measures.The optical sensors used in SLAM are 2D lidar or a 3D lidar, 2D Cameras, Sonar. For the localization, it uses the information from odometry, IMU and GPS.

CONTROL SYSTEMS

CONTROL SYSTEMS

Introduction

Layers of control systems are required to let the robot operate at increasing level of competancy. Many higher level control system as well as lower level control systems are applied together to get a robust and flexible Robot Control System.

What is Control System and PID Controller??

A control system is a device that manages, commands, directs or regulates the behavior of other device or system.There are two common classes of control systems, open loop control systems and closed loop control systems.
PID Controller is one of the most popular and simplest controller widely used.It is a control loop feedback mechanism (controller) that calculates an error value as the difference between a measured process variable and a desired setpoint. The controller attempts to minimize the error by adjusting the process through use of a manipulated variable.

Our Approach

MOTION PLANNING

MOTION PLANNING

Introduction

This is the module that carries the essence of our purpose-autonomous navigation. Motion planning is the process of breaking down a desired movement task into discrete motions that satisfy movement constraints and possibly optimize some aspect of the movement Motion planning can be divided into two:

  1. Path planning
    • using path planning algorithms
    • using machine learning algorithms
  2. Obstacle avoidance
    • using machine learning
    • using sensor data
Building upon the data feed from the sensors and subsequent processing by algorithms,the machine is instructed to move in the desired way.
Our team has been successful in implementing path planning algorithms to navigate our robot autonomously in IGVC 2013 and IGVC 2014.
Due to the huge potential carried by Machine Learning for the self-driving car,,we have separated it out as a separate module.

OUR WORK SO FAR

We have worked on Djkistra’s, A* and Quick Response algorithms for navigation,applying it on binary maps developed using the fusion of 2D Lidar data and processed image-data.
The image processing done on the monocular camera feed to detect lanes and obstacles(static) includes grass removal using machine learning,lane detection using RGB colour thresholding,fixing broken lanes using nearest neighbour algorithm and linear regression.This gives a local target to the planner, i.e - the target direction for movement of the vehicle.This was followed by Inverse Perspective Transform to convert the image into a bird’s eye view.The final image was a binary image.
The fused binary map,referred to as the world map, was then used for path planning.The algorithm chosen finally was A* which takes the target point from the processed data and uses Quick response algorithm to provide maneuverable paths when A* fails in absence of a target point.Additionally,Distance transform was used to make the system more robust. This sums up the path planning module.

FUTURE PLANS

The future aim is to bring in Machine learning intensively into path planning and usin D* Lite algorithm that reduces time complexity considerably.Also intended is to use 3D Lidar.It is to be noted that the Mars rovers Opportunity and Spirit used D* lite for navigation.Also intended is to amalgamate odometry into path planning as a feedback system to know the position,velocity and acceleration of the bot wrt a certain origin.

Wheel Hub Assembly (Rear Wheel) in Eklavya 4.0

Types Of Bearing
  1. Ball Bearings
  2. Thrust Bearings
  3. Roller Bearings

For our design we choose tapered roller bearings Because they are capable of carrying loads in both the directions i.e. axial & radial.It vanishes the necessities of thrust bearings which creating a problem in disassembling.

Tapered roller bearings:

This types of bearings are generally used in all types of vehicles.In our case we uses a pair of tapered roller bearings adjusted in back to back arrangements.

Why back to back?
We can arrange this bearings in 3 ways.
1)Face to face. 2)Back to back. 3)Tandem(parallel).
Face to face have less support width so it doesn’t provide rigid support. These arrangements are less suitable to support tilting moments due to their lower stiffness.

Wheel Hub Assembly[Front Wheel] in Eklavya 4.0 :-

The front wheel is driven by motor.In our design front part doesn’t carry much load.The wheel hub is quite similar to rear but have few changes. Here we use 2 roller bearings. The shaft is made in different steps & have flange welded on it.The steps are given to shaft in order to fix the bearings on it.The monsters from steering column are attached to shaft with the help of bearings. Bearings are fixed by using nut so that they can’t move in axial direction.

Advantages
  1. Easy for assembling & disassembling.
  2. Withstand with very high loads.
  3. Provides rigid support to chassis.
  4. Effectively supports tilting movements.

IGVC 2013

IGVC 2013


Eklavya 2.0


The Team participated in IGVC 2013 using the bot Eklavya 2.0. This vehicle used a more advanced mechanical design and modified software architecture as compared to Eklavya 1.0.
  • Eklavya 2.0 was one of the few bots to use extruded aluminium rods for the chassis(it provides support for fastening all along the rod) and Fiber Reinforced Plastic (FRP) for lamination and water proofing instead of the heavier Perspex.The sheer torque and power was also increased leading to better turns around tight corners.
  • Chain based transmission was implemented for decreasing the loading on the motor shafts.
  • Wheel Odometer was implemented to estimate the position of the robot based on the observed wheel velocities sensed by wheel encoders.
  • Extended Kalman Filter was implemented to improve the accuracy of the localization algorithm beyond the 2.5 m limit set by the GPS/INS system.
  • The entire code base was shifted to the ROS platform, which is the state of the art robotics platform used worldwide.
  • A blob based image filter was implemented in the lidar as part of pre­processing to remove the noise that appeared due to sunlight in an indoor lidar. The low cost indoor LIDAR (an outdoor version costs almost 3 times) was modified so that it could be used in outdoor environments as well as strong sunlight.
  • A robust shadow removal algorithm was implemented which could deal with strong shadows and recover lanes from dark regions in the image.
  • A distance transform based planning algorithm was implemented to create paths that naturally avoids obstacles and reach the target while ensuring that the planner would not get stuck in local minima.
  • The Gazebo simulator was used in parallel to development on the master platform. This helped to figure out high level issues with the code flow, planning and strategy

IGVC 2014

IGVC 2014


Eklavya 3.0


It was used to compete in IGVC 2014. Having tasted success, the team went in for a lot of experimentation this time. Some of the new features of this bot which are listed below:
1. Implementation of Machine Learning algorithm to remove dead grass from the image processed by the camera for better lane detection.
2. The Team performed an Inverse Perspective Transform (IPT) of the camera image to get a bird’s eye view. This allowed us to fuse the Lidar data with the lane data.
3. The vehicle used two cameras at different angles of inclination for better lane detection. This made it possible to remove the blind spot close to the bot.
4. The path planning algorithm was shifted from A-star planning algorithm used in Eklavya 2.0 (based on the availability of the global terrain map) to Quick Reflex planner(a greedy planner, based on local map) in Eklavya 3.0.
5. We also implemented Voronoi edge detection along with distance transform (OpenCV library) to improve the heuristics of A-star and quick reflex planner. The heuristics obtained from Voronoi edge diagrams, replaced the previously used Euclidean and Manhattan heuristics. The implementation of Distance transform in linear time was also one of our key successes in IGVC 2014.

IGVC 2015

The team stood 10th in its group. AGV - IIT Kharagpur was the only team representing India to be able to make it farthest in IGVC-2015 Auto Navigation Challenge at Detroit.
